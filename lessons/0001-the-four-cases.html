The Four Cases

Why German has der / den / dem / des — and how to pick the right one without freezing mid-sentence.

Mission link. You're sharpening shaky foundations on the way to B2/C1 for living and working in German. The case system is the keystone: get it solid and articles, adjective endings, pronouns and prepositions all start to click. This lesson does one thing — recognise the case and choose the correct article. ~20–25 minutes.

iThe one idea

In English, word order tells you who does what: "The dog bites the man" ≠ "The man bites the dog." German instead marks the job each noun does by changing its article. That job is called the case (Kasus). Because the case carries the meaning, German can move words around far more freely.

Watch one sentence carry three different jobs:

Der Mann gibt der Frau den Schlüssel.

The man gives the woman the key.

NOM der Mann — the giver (subject)
wer gibt? → der Mann
DAT der Frau — the receiver
wem? → der Frau
AKK den Schlüssel — the thing given
wen/was? → den Schlüssel
GEN possession: des Mannes
wessen? → of the man
The test questions are your best friend: wer?→Nom, wen?→Akk, wem?→Dat, wessen?→Gen. Ask them about the noun and the case falls out.

iiThe table you actually need

Definite article ("the"). Don't memorise 16 boxes cold — see the pattern:

Casemasc.fem.neut.plural
NOM wer?derdiedasdie
AKK wen?dendiedasdie
DAT wem?demderdemden
GEN wessen?desderdesder
80/20 shortcut: feminine, neuter and plural barely change between Nom and Akk. The real movement is the masculine column (der → den → dem → des) and the dative row. Nail those two and you've covered most real sentences.
Two noun tweaks: dative plural adds -n (mit den Kindern), and genitive masc/neut nouns add -s/-es (des Mannes).
