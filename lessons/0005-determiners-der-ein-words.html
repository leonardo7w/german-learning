Determiners: dieser, jeder, kein, mein…

The little words before the noun — dieser, jeder, welcher, alle, kein, mein, eigen. Learn which family each belongs to, and the endings fall out of what you already know.

Mission link. These words are everywhere in real German — diese Woche, jeden Tag, mein eigenes Büro, alle Kollegen, welcher Zug. The good news: they're not new grammar, they're the same case + ending system from Lessons 01–02 wearing different clothes. This lesson ties it all together. ~20–25 min.

↻Warm-up: cases (30 seconds)

Quick recall before we build — these are the cases you said still wobble. (New: every lesson now opens with one of these.)

iTwo families, that's the whole secret

Every determiner is either a der-WORD or an ein-WORD.
That single fact tells you its endings and the adjective endings after it.
der-WORDS — decline like der / die / das
dieser (this) · jeder (each) · jener (that) · welcher (which) · mancher (some) · solcher (such) · alle (all, pl) · beide (both, pl)
Same endings as the definite article → trigger WEAK adjective endings after them.
ein-WORDS — decline like ein / kein
ein · kein (no/not a) · mein, dein, sein, ihr, unser, euer, Ihr (possessives) · irgendein
Same endings as the indefinite article → trigger MIXED adjective endings after them.
"eigen" (own) is a trap: it is not a determiner — it's an ordinary adjective. So it takes a normal adjective ending: mein eigenes Büro (mixed, neut), in meinem eigenen Büro (mixed, dat).

Number quirks: jeder is singular only ("each"); its plural is alle. jeden Tag → alle Tage. And alle / diese / meine + adjective → the adjective is plural (mostly -en): alle neuen Kollegen.
