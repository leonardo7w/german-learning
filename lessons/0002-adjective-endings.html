Adjective Endings

When is it der gute Code, ein guter Code, or just guter Code? The one rule that decides all of it.

Mission link. In Lesson 01 you learned the four cases. Adjective endings are where cases bite: every time you describe something — ein erfahrener Architekt, mit agilen Methoden — you must pick the right ending live. This is the single most common place intermediate speakers wobble. Master the one rule below and you stop guessing. ~25 minutes, and the exercises ramp up to B2 difficulty.

iThe one rule

The gender/case marker (the -r / -e / -s / -m / -n signal)
must appear exactly once in the phrase —
on the article or on the adjective. Never both, never neither.

That single principle generates all three "declensions". Ask: does the article already show the case & gender clearly?

WEAK Article does the job (after der/die/das, dieser, jeder, welche, alle…). The adjective relaxes → only -e or -en.
der gute Code · dem guten Code

MIXED Article is half-blind (after ein/kein/mein, dein, sein, ihr, unser…). "ein" shows nothing for masc-nom & neut; the adjective patches the gap with the strong ending there, else weak.
ein guter Code · ein gutes Team · einen guten Code

STRONG No article at all (or after etwas, viel, wenig, numbers). The adjective carries the full signal — it borrows the der/die/das endings.
guter Code · kaltes Wasser · mit gutem Code

iiiThe 4-step procedure

For every adjective, run this in your head:

1. What's the article? der-word → WEAK · ein-word → MIXED · none → STRONG.
2. What case? (subject? object? after a preposition? → use Lesson 01.)
3. What gender/number is the noun?
4. Read the ending off the right table.

EBoss level: prepositions decide the case

Now the article doesn't hand you the case — a preposition does. Recall Lesson 01: mit/bei/aus/zu → Dat; für/durch/ohne → Akk; in/auf/an → Dat (location) or Akk (motion). Then pick the ending. B1–B2
